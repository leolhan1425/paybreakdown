## Quick Answer

**Net pay is the amount of money you actually receive in your paycheck after all taxes and deductions are subtracted from your gross pay.** Also called take-home pay, this is the number that hits your bank account on payday. For most workers, net pay is 25-35% less than gross pay, depending on your tax bracket, state, and deductions.

## How Net Pay Is Calculated

Net pay starts with your [gross pay](/learn/what-is-gross-pay/) and subtracts every withholding and deduction. The formula is simple:

**Gross Pay - Taxes - Deductions = Net Pay**

The deductions fall into two main groups:

### Mandatory Deductions (Taxes)
- **Federal income tax**: Based on your [tax bracket](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/) and [W-4](/learn/what-is-a-w4/) elections
- **State income tax**: Varies by state; some states like [Texas](/texas/) and [Florida](/florida/) charge zero
- **Social Security tax**: [6.2% of wages](/learn/what-is-social-security-tax/) up to $176,100 in 2025
- **Medicare tax**: [1.45% of all wages](/learn/what-is-medicare-tax/), plus an additional 0.9% on earnings above $200,000
- **Local taxes**: Some cities and counties impose [local income taxes](/learn/local-income-taxes/)

### Voluntary Deductions
- 401(k) or [403(b) contributions](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/)
- Health, dental, and vision insurance premiums
- [HSA](/learn/what-is-an-hsa/) or [FSA](/learn/what-is-an-fsa/) contributions
- Life insurance or disability insurance
- Union dues

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Michael earns $85,000 per year in [California](/california/). He is single, contributes 5% to his 401(k), and pays $150 per paycheck for health insurance. Here is his biweekly breakdown:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross pay (biweekly) | $3,269.23 |
| Federal income tax | -$420.00 |
| California state tax | -$185.00 |
| Social Security (6.2%) | -$202.69 |
| Medicare (1.45%) | -$47.40 |
| CA SDI (1.1%) | -$35.96 |
| 401(k) (5%) | -$163.46 |
| Health insurance | -$150.00 |
| **Net pay** | **$2,064.72** |

Michael keeps about 63% of his gross pay. Someone earning the same salary in Texas would take home roughly $200 more per paycheck because there is no state income tax. ## Why Net Pay Varies So Much

Two people earning the same $85,000 salary can have very different net pay amounts. The biggest factors are:

- **State of residence**: The gap between [high-tax states](/learn/highest-tax-states/) and [no-tax states](/learn/states-with-no-income-tax/) can be over $5,000 per year
- **Filing status**: [Married filing jointly](/learn/married-filing-jointly-explained/) often results in lower tax rates than [single filing](/learn/single-vs-married-filing/)
- **Pre-tax deductions**: Higher [401(k) contributions](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) and [HSA contributions](/learn/what-is-an-hsa/) lower your taxable income, which counterintuitively means less tax is withheld
- **W-4 elections**: How you fill out your [W-4 form](/learn/what-is-a-w4/) directly controls how much federal tax is withheld each paycheck

## What to Do With This Number

Your net pay is the real number for budgeting. When calculating whether you can [afford rent](/afford/) or planning monthly expenses, always use net pay — not your gross salary. Financial advisors generally recommend spending no more than 30% of your net pay on housing.

## Quick Answer

**Medicare tax is 1.45% of your gross wages with no income cap, and your employer pays a matching 1.45%.** Combined, 2.9% of your wages fund Medicare. If you earn more than $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly), you also pay an [Additional Medicare Tax](/learn/what-is-additional-medicare-tax/) of 0.9% on wages above those thresholds. Medicare tax is part of [FICA](/learn/what-is-fica/) and is withheld from every paycheck automatically.

## How Medicare Tax Works

Medicare tax is the second component of [FICA](/learn/what-is-fica/), alongside [Social Security tax](/learn/what-is-social-security-tax/). The key difference is that Medicare has no wage cap — every dollar you earn is subject to the 1.45% tax.

### 2025 Medicare Tax Rates

| Category | Rate | Applies To |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Employee | 1.45% | All wages |
| Employer | 1.45% | All wages |
| Combined | 2.9% | All wages |
| Additional Medicare Tax | 0.9% | Wages above $200,000 (single) |

The Additional Medicare Tax is paid only by the employee — employers do not match it. Learn more about [when it applies](/learn/what-is-additional-medicare-tax/).

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Sophia earns $85,000 in [California](/california/) and is paid semimonthly (24 paychecks per year).

| Detail | Annual | Per Paycheck |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Gross pay | $85,000 | $3,541.67 |
| Medicare tax (1.45%) | $1,232.50 | $51.35 |
| Employer match (1.45%) | $1,232.50 | $51.35 |
| **Total Medicare funding** | **$2,465.00** | **$102.71** |

Sophia's $51.35 per paycheck goes toward Medicare along with her employer's match. She does not hit the $200,000 threshold, so no Additional Medicare Tax applies.

Now consider Miguel, who earns $250,000. His Medicare tax:
- 1.45% on all $250,000 = $3,625.00
- Additional 0.9% on $50,000 ($250,000 - $200,000) = $450.00
- **Total Medicare tax: $4,075.00**

## Medicare Tax vs Social Security Tax

| Feature | Medicare | Social Security |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| Employee rate | 1.45% | 6.2% |
| Wage cap | None | $176,100 (2025) |
| Additional surcharge | 0.9% above $200K | None |
| What it funds | Hospital insurance | Retirement, disability, survivors |

Because Social Security caps at $176,100 while Medicare continues on all earnings, very high earners pay a larger share of their [FICA](/learn/what-is-fica/) in Medicare relative to Social Security.

## Medicare Tax for Self-Employed

[Self-employed workers](/learn/what-is-self-employment-tax/) pay both the employee and employer portions — the full 2.9% on net self-employment income. The Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% also applies to self-employment income above $200,000. Half of the 2.9% base Medicare tax is deductible as an above-the-line adjustment to [AGI](/learn/what-is-agi/).

See how self-employment taxes compare to W-2 employment with the [freelance calculator](/freelance/) or read the [1099 vs W-2 comparison](/learn/1099-vs-w2-explained/).

## Can You Reduce Medicare Tax?

There is almost nothing you can do to reduce Medicare tax on wages. Unlike federal income tax, contributions to a [401(k)](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/), [traditional IRA](/learn/traditional-vs-roth-ira/), or the [standard deduction](/learn/what-is-standard-deduction/) do not lower your Medicare tax. The only payroll deductions that reduce Medicare tax are those made through a Section 125 cafeteria plan, such as [FSA](/learn/what-is-an-fsa/) and employer-sponsored [HSA](/learn/what-is-an-hsa/) contributions.

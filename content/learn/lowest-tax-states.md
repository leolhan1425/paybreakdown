## Quick Answer

**The lowest tax states in America combine no or low income tax with moderate sales and property taxes.** For most workers, Wyoming, Alaska, Nevada, Florida, and South Dakota offer the lightest overall tax burden. However, the best state for you depends on your income, whether you own or rent, and your spending patterns. Income tax is not the only factor — [property tax and sales tax](/learn/how-state-taxes-work/) matter too.

## Lowest Tax States for Different Situations

### For Renters
No-income-tax states with moderate sales tax are the best deal. Wyoming, Nevada, and Florida stand out because you avoid income tax and do not pay property tax directly.

### For Homeowners
Property tax matters enormously. Nevada (0.53%) and Wyoming (0.55%) combine no income tax with low property tax. Texas has no income tax but high property tax (1.6%), which can cost $5,000-$8,000/year on a typical home.

### For Retirees
Florida and Tennessee are popular because they have no income tax on retirement distributions, Social Security, or pensions, plus relatively low property taxes. See [how taxes change in retirement](/learn/taxes-when-you-retire/).

### For High Earners
The [no-income-tax states](/learn/states-with-no-income-tax/) save the most at high incomes. Someone earning $300,000 in California pays over $20,000 in state income tax alone. Moving to Florida eliminates that entirely.

## The Trade-Offs of Low-Tax States

Low taxes often mean:
- **Fewer public services**: Smaller state budgets may mean less funding for public schools, parks, and infrastructure
- **Higher out-of-pocket costs**: You may pay more for services that are tax-funded in other states
- **Limited job markets**: Wyoming and South Dakota have smaller economies and fewer career options
- **Weather and lifestyle**: Tax savings are only one factor in quality of life

## Quick Answer

**FICA stands for the Federal Insurance Contributions Act, and it funds Social Security and Medicare.** Every paycheck, 7.65% of your gross pay goes to FICA — 6.2% for [Social Security](/learn/what-is-social-security-tax/) and 1.45% for [Medicare](/learn/what-is-medicare-tax/). Your employer pays a matching 7.65%, so the total contribution is 15.3% of your wages. Unlike federal income tax, FICA has no brackets, no deductions, and no way to reduce it through [401(k) contributions](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) or other pre-tax benefits.

## How FICA Tax Works

FICA is a flat tax applied to your [gross pay](/learn/what-is-gross-pay/) from the first dollar. There is no [standard deduction](/learn/what-is-standard-deduction/) or personal exemption for FICA — every dollar you earn is subject to it (up to the Social Security cap).

### The Two Components

| Component | Rate | Wage Cap (2025) |
|-----------|------|-----------------|
| [Social Security](/learn/what-is-social-security-tax/) | 6.2% | $176,100 |
| [Medicare](/learn/what-is-medicare-tax/) | 1.45% | No cap |
| [Additional Medicare](/learn/what-is-additional-medicare-tax/) | 0.9% | On wages above $200,000 |

Once your earnings exceed $176,100 in a calendar year, you stop paying the Social Security portion. Medicare has no cap — you pay 1.45% on every dollar, plus an extra 0.9% on earnings above $200,000.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Emma earns $70,000 per year in [Florida](/florida/). Here is her annual FICA breakdown:

| Tax | Calculation | Annual Amount | Per Biweekly Paycheck |
|-----|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Social Security | $70,000 x 6.2% | $4,340.00 | $166.92 |
| Medicare | $70,000 x 1.45% | $1,015.00 | $39.04 |
| **Total FICA** | | **$5,355.00** | **$205.96** |

Emma's employer also pays $5,355 in matching FICA, bringing the total Social Security and Medicare funding from her wages to $10,710. Use the [SalaryHog calculator](/) to see FICA broken out on your paycheck.

## FICA vs Federal Income Tax

| | FICA | Federal Income Tax |
|---|---|---|
| Rate | Flat 7.65% | [Progressive](/learn/what-is-progressive-tax/) 10-37% |
| Deductions reduce it? | No | Yes |
| Applies to all income? | Wages only | All taxable income |
| Cap? | Social Security capped at $176,100 | No cap |

This is why FICA can feel disproportionately heavy for lower and middle-income earners. Someone making $50,000 pays 7.65% in FICA on every dollar. Someone making $500,000 pays a lower effective FICA rate because the Social Security portion stops at $176,100.

## FICA for Self-Employed Workers

If you are [self-employed](/learn/what-is-self-employment-tax/), there is no employer to pay the matching half. You owe the full 15.3% yourself, known as [self-employment tax](/learn/what-is-self-employment-tax/). The good news is you can deduct half of that amount when calculating your [AGI](/learn/what-is-agi/). Compare [1099 vs W-2](/learn/1099-vs-w2-explained/) to see the full tax difference, or try the [freelance calculator](/freelance/).

## Can You Reduce FICA?

Unlike income tax, there are very few ways to lower your FICA bill:

- **[HSA contributions](/learn/what-is-an-hsa/) through payroll**: If your employer deducts HSA contributions pre-FICA (via a Section 125 plan), those dollars avoid FICA
- **[FSA contributions](/learn/what-is-an-fsa/)**: Same as HSA when done through payroll
- **Employer retirement contributions**: Your employer's [401(k) match](/learn/employer-match-explained/) is not subject to FICA

Note that your own 401(k) contributions do NOT reduce FICA — they only reduce federal and state income tax. Check your [pay stub](/learn/how-to-read-your-pay-stub/) to see FICA itemized separately.

## Quick Answer

**A flat tax charges everyone the same rate regardless of income, while a progressive tax charges higher rates as income increases.** The U.S. federal income tax is progressive with rates from 10% to 37%. Several states use a flat tax instead, charging a single rate on all taxable income. Both systems have trade-offs — flat taxes are simpler but take a proportionally larger bite from lower earners; progressive taxes target ability to pay but are more complex.

## How Each System Works

### Progressive Tax (Federal System)
Income is divided into [brackets](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/), each taxed at an increasing rate. Your first $11,925 is taxed at 10%, the next chunk at 12%, then 22%, and so on up to 37%. Your [effective tax rate](/learn/what-is-effective-tax-rate/) falls somewhere between the lowest and highest brackets.

### Flat Tax (Some State Systems)
Every dollar of taxable income is taxed at the same rate. If a state has a 5% flat tax, someone earning $30,000 and someone earning $300,000 both pay 5% of their taxable income.

## Real Example Comparing Both Systems

Let's compare how each system treats two workers — one earning $40,000 and another earning $150,000. We'll use the 2025 federal progressive brackets and a hypothetical 15% flat tax:

### Progressive Tax (Taxable Income After $15,000 Standard Deduction)

**Worker A**: $40,000 salary, $25,000 taxable income
- Federal tax: $2,762 — Effective rate: **11.0%**

**Worker B**: $150,000 salary, $135,000 taxable income
- Federal tax: $24,118 — Effective rate: **17.9%**

### Flat Tax at 15%

**Worker A**: $25,000 x 15% = $3,750 — Effective rate: **15.0%**

**Worker B**: $135,000 x 15% = $20,250 — Effective rate: **15.0%**

Under a flat tax, Worker A pays $988 more while Worker B pays $3,868 less. This illustrates the core trade-off. Run your own progressive tax numbers at the [SalaryHog calculator](/).

## Pros and Cons

### Flat Tax
- **Pro**: Simple to calculate and understand
- **Pro**: No bracket creep from inflation
- **Con**: Lower earners pay the same rate as wealthy taxpayers
- **Con**: Can be regressive when combined with other flat taxes (sales tax, payroll tax)

### Progressive Tax
- **Pro**: Accounts for ability to pay
- **Pro**: Lower rates protect lower earners
- **Con**: More complex, requires bracket tables
- **Con**: Can create misconceptions about [how raises are taxed](/learn/how-raises-are-taxed/)

## What This Means for You

If you are comparing jobs in different states, the type of tax system matters. A flat-tax state is predictable — you know exactly what percentage goes to state tax. A progressive-tax state might charge you more or less depending on your income level.

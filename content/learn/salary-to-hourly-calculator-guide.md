## Quick Answer

**To convert a salary to an hourly rate, divide the annual salary by 2,080 (40 hours/week x 52 weeks).** A $70,000 salary equals $33.65/hour. However, if you regularly work more than 40 hours per week, your effective hourly rate is lower. Understanding your true hourly rate helps you evaluate job offers, compare positions, and decide whether overtime or a second job is worth your time.

## The Basic Formula

**Annual Salary / 2,080 = Hourly Rate**

| Annual Salary | Hourly Rate |
|--------------|-------------|
| $35,000 | $16.83 |
| $45,000 | $21.63 |
| $55,000 | $26.44 |

| $150,000 | $72.12 |

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Rebecca earns $72,000 as a project manager in [California](/california/).

**Standard hourly rate**: $72,000 / 2,080 = **$34.62/hour**

But Rebecca typically works 45 hours per week:

**Effective hourly rate**: $72,000 / (45 x 52) = **$30.77/hour**

Those extra 5 hours per week reduce her effective rate by nearly $4/hour. If she factors in her 3-week commute time per year, the rate drops further.

Now consider her take-home rate. After taxes in California:
- Federal tax + FICA + CA state tax + SDI = approximately $19,500
- [Take-home pay](/learn/what-is-net-pay/): $72,000 - $19,500 = $52,500
- **Take-home hourly rate**: $52,500 / 2,080 = **$25.24/hour**

That $34.62 headline rate becomes $25.24 after taxes — and even less if she works extra hours. See your take-home hourly rate at the [SalaryHog calculator](/).

## Why Your Effective Hourly Rate Matters

### Comparing Job Offers
A salaried position at $75,000 with 45-hour weeks ($32.05/hour effective) may be worse than an hourly position at $33/hour with strict 40-hour weeks ($68,640/year). Compare [salary vs total compensation](/learn/salary-vs-total-compensation/) before deciding.

### Evaluating Overtime
If your effective hourly rate is $30.77 and a side gig pays $40/hour, the side gig is clearly worth your time. But if your salary translates to $48/hour and the gig pays $40, you might be better off working for your employer.

### Freelance Rate Setting
When transitioning from W-2 to [freelance/1099 work](/learn/1099-vs-w2-explained/), multiply your W-2 hourly rate by 1.3-1.5 to account for self-employment tax, insurance, and missing benefits. A $35/hour W-2 rate should translate to at least $45-$53/hour as a [freelancer](/freelance/).

## Accounting for Benefits

Your salary is only part of your compensation. To calculate your true hourly compensation including benefits:

| Benefit | Annual Value |
|---------|-------------|
| Health insurance (employer portion) | $7,000-$15,000 |
| [401(k) match](/learn/employer-match-explained/) (4% on $72,000) | $2,880 |
| PTO (3 weeks) | $4,154 |
| Other benefits (disability, life insurance) | $1,000-$3,000 |
| **Total compensation** | **$87,034-$96,034** |

Adjusted hourly rate with benefits: $87,034 / 2,080 = **$41.84/hour**

That is significantly more than the $34.62 base rate. See [salary vs total compensation](/learn/salary-vs-total-compensation/) for more details.

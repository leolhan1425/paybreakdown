## Quick Answer

**About a dozen states use a flat (single-rate) income tax instead of progressive brackets.** Rates range from around 2.5% (Arizona) to 5.0% (Massachusetts). Flat taxes are simpler to calculate — you multiply your taxable income by one rate — but they take the same percentage from everyone regardless of income. Whether a flat-tax state is "better" depends on your income level and what you would pay under a progressive system.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Compare two workers earning $90,000 — one in Illinois (flat 4.95%) and one in [California](/california/) (progressive up to 13.3%).

**Illinois (Flat)**:
- State taxable income: ~$90,000
- State tax: $90,000 x 4.95% = **$4,455**

**California (Progressive)**:
- State taxable income: ~$90,000
- State tax: approximately **$4,200** (progressive rates, with lower brackets taxed at 1-6%)

At $90,000, the difference is modest — Illinois is actually slightly higher. But at $200,000:

- **Illinois**: $200,000 x 4.95% = $9,900
- **California**: approximately $14,500 (higher progressive rates kick in)

Flat taxes favor higher earners, while progressive taxes are gentler on lower incomes. See your specific state at the [SalaryHog calculator](/).

## Flat Tax Pros and Cons

### Advantages
- **Simplicity**: One rate, easy to calculate and plan around
- **Predictability**: You always know your exact state tax percentage
- **No bracket creep**: Raises are taxed at the same rate as existing income
- **Competitive rates**: Many flat-tax states have set rates lower than the top progressive rates in neighboring states

### Disadvantages
- **Same rate for all incomes**: Someone earning $25,000 pays the same percentage as someone earning $250,000
- **Effectively regressive**: When combined with sales tax and property tax, flat income taxes can mean a higher total tax burden on lower earners
- **No progressive relief**: No lower rate on your first dollars of income like the federal system provides

## Trend Toward Flat Taxes

Several states have recently converted from progressive brackets to flat rates, including Iowa, Georgia, and Mississippi. The argument for conversion is simplicity and competitiveness with neighboring states. Critics argue it shifts the tax burden toward lower-income residents.

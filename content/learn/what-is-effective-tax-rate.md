## Quick Answer

**Your effective tax rate is the average percentage of your income that you actually pay in taxes.** It is always lower than your [marginal tax rate](/learn/what-is-marginal-tax-rate/) because the progressive system taxes your first dollars at lower rates. If you are in the 22% tax bracket, your effective federal rate might only be 13-16%. The effective rate gives you the truest picture of your overall tax burden.

## How to Calculate Your Effective Tax Rate

The formula is straightforward:

**Effective Tax Rate = Total Tax Owed / Total Income**

You can calculate this using either your taxable income or your gross income, depending on what you want to measure. Using taxable income shows your rate after deductions; using gross income shows your total burden including the benefit of deductions.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Maria is single, earns $90,000, and lives in [Florida](/florida/) (no state income tax). She takes the [standard deduction](/learn/what-is-standard-deduction/) of $15,000, giving her a taxable income of $75,000.

Federal tax calculation across [brackets](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/):

| Bracket | Income | Tax |
|---------|--------|-----|
| 10% | $11,925 | $1,192.50 |
| 12% | $36,550 | $4,386.00 |
| 22% | $26,525 ($75,000 - $48,475) | $5,835.50 |
| **Total** | **$75,000** | **$11,414.00** |

Maria's numbers:
- **Marginal rate**: 22% (the bracket her last dollar falls in)
- **Effective rate on taxable income**: 15.2% ($11,414 / $75,000)
- **Effective rate on gross income**: 12.7% ($11,414 / $90,000)

The 22% marginal rate sounds high, but Maria is effectively paying 12.7% of her gross income in federal tax. Add [FICA taxes](/learn/what-is-fica/) (7.65%) and her total effective federal rate is about 20.4%. Run your own numbers with the [SalaryHog calculator](/).

## Effective Rate vs Marginal Rate

These two rates answer different questions:

| | Effective Rate | Marginal Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Measures | Average tax on all income | Tax on the next dollar earned |
| Use it for | Budgeting, comparing total burden | Evaluating deductions, side income |
| Maria's example | 15.2% | 22% |

When someone asks "what percent of my income goes to taxes?" they want the effective rate. When deciding whether to contribute more to a [401(k)](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) or pick up [freelance work](/freelance/), the marginal rate is more relevant.

## Why Effective Rates Vary Widely

Two people earning the same salary can have very different effective rates:

- **Deductions**: Someone maximizing their [401(k)](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) and [HSA](/learn/what-is-an-hsa/) contributions has less taxable income and a lower effective rate
- **Filing status**: A couple [filing jointly](/learn/married-filing-jointly-explained/) on $150,000 combined income pays a lower effective rate than a single person earning $150,000
- **State taxes**: Maria in Florida pays 0% state tax. The same salary in [California](/california/) would add roughly 5-6% to her total effective rate
- **Dependents**: [Claiming dependents](/learn/how-dependents-affect-taxes/) provides credits that directly reduce tax owed

## How to Lower Your Effective Tax Rate

The most common strategies include:

1. **Maximize pre-tax retirement contributions** — [Traditional 401(k)](/learn/traditional-vs-roth-401k/) and [Traditional IRA](/learn/traditional-vs-roth-ira/) contributions reduce taxable income
2. **Use tax-advantaged accounts** — [HSA](/learn/what-is-an-hsa/) and [FSA](/learn/what-is-an-fsa/) contributions lower your taxable income
3. **Choose the right filing status** — Compare [single vs married filing](/learn/single-vs-married-filing/) to find the lower rate
4. **Consider relocation** — Moving from a high-tax to a [no-tax state](/learn/states-with-no-income-tax/) can significantly cut your total effective rate. Use the [relocation tool](/relocate/) to estimate the difference.

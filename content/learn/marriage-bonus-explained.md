## Quick Answer

**The marriage bonus is the tax savings a couple gets by filing jointly instead of as two single filers.** It occurs most often when one spouse earns significantly more than the other. The higher earner benefits from the lower earner's unused [tax bracket](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/) space and the doubled [standard deduction](/learn/what-is-standard-deduction/), resulting in a lower combined tax bill than if both filed separately as single.

## How the Marriage Bonus Works

When you file as [married filing jointly](/learn/married-filing-jointly-explained/), your combined income is spread across bracket thresholds that are double the single amounts (for most brackets). If one spouse earns much more than the other, the high earner's income that would have been in the 22% or 24% bracket as a single filer drops into the 12% bracket on the joint return because the other spouse's bracket space is unused.

Additionally, the joint standard deduction is $30,000 vs $15,000 for a single filer. A couple where only one person works gets $30,000 in deductions on the earner's income — double what they would get filing as single.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Andrew earns $140,000 and his wife Beth is a stay-at-home parent. They live in [Texas](/texas/).

### If Andrew Filed as Single
- Taxable income: $140,000 - $15,000 = $125,000
- Federal tax: $1,192.50 + $4,386 + $12,072.50 + $5,196 = **$22,847**

### Filing Jointly
- Taxable income: $140,000 - $30,000 = $110,000
- Federal tax: $2,385 + $8,772 + $2,871 = **$14,028**

**Marriage bonus: $8,819 per year**

Andrew saves nearly $9,000 in federal taxes by being married and filing jointly. The extra $15,000 standard deduction and Beth's unused bracket space make a huge difference. Try the [married calculator](/married/) to see your bonus.

## When the Marriage Bonus Is Largest

The bonus grows with greater income disparity between spouses:

| Earner 1 | Earner 2 | Combined Tax (Joint) | Tax as Two Singles | Marriage Bonus |
|----------|----------|---------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| $100,000 | $0 | $8,370 | $14,260 | $5,890 |
| $150,000 | $0 | $14,028 | $24,458 | $10,430 |
| $200,000 | $0 | $29,566 | $38,878 | $9,312 |
| $100,000 | $50,000 | $14,996 | $17,622 | $2,626 |
| $100,000 | $100,000 | $21,400 | $21,400 | $0 |

When both spouses earn the same amount, the bonus disappears entirely. When incomes are very different, the bonus can exceed $10,000.

## The Marriage Bonus Beyond Federal Tax

### State Taxes
In states with [progressive taxes](/learn/how-state-taxes-work/), the marriage bonus can add additional savings. [California](/california/) and [New York](/new-york/) have their own bracket structures for joint filers. States with [no income tax](/learn/states-with-no-income-tax/) like [Texas](/texas/) and [Florida](/florida/) have no state-level bonus to gain.

### Credits and Deductions
Filing jointly also opens up credits that increase the effective bonus:
- [Roth IRA](/learn/traditional-vs-roth-ira/) contribution phaseout is higher for joint filers
- Earned Income Tax Credit is available (not available for [MFS](/learn/married-filing-separately-explained/))
- Education credits have higher AGI phaseouts
- [Child Tax Credit](/learn/how-dependents-affect-taxes/) applies more favorably

## Getting Married? Here Is What to Do

If you are planning to get married and expect a marriage bonus:

1. **Update your [W-4](/learn/what-is-a-w4/)** after the wedding to adjust [withholding](/learn/what-is-tax-withholding/)
2. **Coordinate retirement contributions** — Both spouses should maximize [401(k)](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) contributions
3. **Plan for the first joint return** — If you marry in December, you are considered married for the entire year
4. **Review state tax impact** — Check [how your state handles joint filing](/learn/how-state-taxes-work/)

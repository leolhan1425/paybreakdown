## Quick Answer

**Independent contractors pay both income tax and a 15.3% [self-employment tax](/learn/what-is-self-employment-tax/) on their net earnings, with no employer to withhold taxes or split FICA costs.** You are responsible for making [quarterly estimated payments](/learn/quarterly-estimated-taxes/), tracking all expenses for [deductions](/learn/freelancer-tax-deductions/), and filing [Schedule C](/learn/what-is-schedule-c/) with your annual return. A good rule of thumb is to set aside 25-30% of your gross income for federal taxes.

## Your Tax Obligations as an Independent Contractor

### 1. Federal Income Tax
Your net self-employment income (gross income minus [business expenses](/learn/freelancer-tax-deductions/)) is added to any other income on your Form 1040 and taxed through the normal [progressive brackets](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/).

### 2. Self-Employment Tax
You owe 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on 92.35% of your net SE income. This replaces the [FICA taxes](/learn/what-is-fica/) that W-2 employees split with their employer. See [how SE tax works](/learn/what-is-self-employment-tax/).

### 3. State Income Tax
If you live in a state with income tax, your contractor income is subject to state tax too. States like [Texas](/texas/) and [Florida](/florida/) charge nothing; [California](/california/) and [New York](/new-york/) charge up to 13.3% and 10.9% respectively.

### 4. Quarterly Estimated Payments
Without an employer to withhold, you must send [quarterly payments](/learn/quarterly-estimated-taxes/) to the IRS (and your state) four times per year.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Amanda is an independent UX designer in [California](/california/) earning $105,000 from three clients. She has $18,000 in business expenses.

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross 1099 income | $105,000 |
| Business expenses | -$18,000 |
| **Net self-employment income** | **$87,000** |
| SE tax base (92.35%) | $80,344.50 |
| **Self-employment tax** | **$12,293** |
| SE deduction (half) | -$6,146 |
| AGI | $80,854 |
| Standard deduction | -$15,000 |
| QBI deduction (est.) | -$13,000 |
| **Taxable income** | **$52,854** |
| Federal income tax | ~$6,850 |
| California state tax | ~$3,400 |
| CA SDI | ~$957 |
| **Total annual tax** | **~$23,500** |
| **Take-home pay** | **~$63,500** (60.5% of gross) |

Amanda's [quarterly estimated payment](/learn/quarterly-estimated-taxes/) should be about **$5,875** to the IRS plus **$1,090** to California. Use the [freelance calculator](/freelance/) to estimate your payments.

## Essential Deductions for Independent Contractors

Track these expenses to reduce your tax bill:

- **[Home office](/learn/home-office-deduction/)**: $1,500 (simplified) or more (regular method)
- **Equipment and technology**: Computers, cameras, tools
- **Software**: Subscriptions, cloud services
- **Professional development**: Courses, certifications, books
- **Mileage**: 67 cents/mile in 2025
- **Health insurance**: 100% of premiums (above-the-line deduction)
- **Retirement**: Solo 401(k) up to $69,000 total or SEP-IRA up to 25% of net SE income

See the [complete deduction list](/learn/freelancer-tax-deductions/).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not saving enough for taxes**: Set aside 25-30% of every payment immediately
2. **Missing quarterly payments**: Results in penalties even if you pay in full by April 15
3. **Not tracking expenses**: Every untracked deduction is money lost. See [Schedule C guide](/learn/what-is-schedule-c/)
4. **Confusing gross with net**: Your 1099 shows gross payments. You report NET income (gross minus expenses)
5. **Not considering business structure**: If you earn over $70,000, an [S-Corp election](/learn/sole-proprietor-vs-llc-taxes/) or [LLC](/learn/how-to-pay-yourself-as-business-owner/) could save on SE tax

## Quick Answer

**Gross pay is the total amount of money you earn before any taxes or deductions are taken out.** It includes your base salary or hourly wages, plus any bonuses, overtime, commissions, or other compensation. Gross pay is always higher than your [net pay (take-home pay)](/learn/what-is-net-pay/) because taxes and deductions haven't been subtracted yet.

## How Gross Pay Works

When your employer agrees to pay you $60,000 per year, that number is your gross annual pay. But you never actually receive that full amount in your bank account. Before your paycheck reaches you, your employer withholds federal income tax, state income tax (in most states), Social Security tax, Medicare tax, and any voluntary deductions like 401(k) contributions or health insurance premiums.

Gross pay is the starting point for every tax calculation. The IRS, your state tax agency, and payroll processors all begin with your gross pay to determine how much you owe.

## Calculating Gross Pay

For **salaried employees**, the math is straightforward:

- Annual gross pay / number of pay periods = gross pay per paycheck
- $60,000 / 26 (biweekly) = $2,307.69 per paycheck
- $60,000 / 24 (semimonthly) = $2,500 per paycheck

For **hourly employees**, gross pay depends on hours worked:

- Hourly rate x hours worked = gross pay
- $25/hour x 80 hours (biweekly) = $2,000 gross pay

If you work overtime, those hours are typically paid at 1.5x your regular rate, and that additional amount is added to your gross pay. Learn more about [how overtime is taxed](/learn/how-overtime-is-taxed/).

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Sarah earns a $75,000 salary in [Texas](/texas/) (no state income tax). Her biweekly gross pay is $2,884.62. Here is what happens next:

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Gross pay | $2,884.62 |
| Federal income tax | -$354.00 |
| Social Security (6.2%) | -$178.85 |
| Medicare (1.45%) | -$41.83 |
| 401(k) contribution (6%) | -$173.08 |
| Health insurance | -$125.00 |
| **Net pay (take-home)** | **$2,011.86** |

Sarah's gross pay is $2,884.62, but she takes home $2,011.86. That is a roughly 30% difference. You can run your own numbers using the [SalaryHog calculator](/) to see your estimated gross-to-net breakdown.

## Why Gross Pay Matters

Understanding gross pay is essential for several reasons:

- **Tax planning**: Your [tax bracket](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/) is based on your gross income minus deductions, not your take-home pay.
- **Loan applications**: Lenders typically ask for your gross annual income when you apply for a mortgage or car loan. Check if you can [afford rent](/afford/) based on your gross pay.
- **Salary comparisons**: When comparing job offers or [negotiating salary](/learn/negotiating-salary-tips/), the numbers discussed are almost always gross pay.
- **Benefits enrollment**: Many employer benefits like [401(k) contributions](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) are calculated as a percentage of gross pay.

## Gross Pay vs Gross Income

These terms are often used interchangeably, but there is a subtle difference. Gross pay specifically refers to employment earnings, while [gross annual income](/learn/what-is-gross-annual-income/) can include other sources like investment returns, rental income, or side hustle earnings. When filing your taxes, all income sources combine to form your total gross income, which is then used to calculate your [adjusted gross income (AGI)](/learn/what-is-agi/).

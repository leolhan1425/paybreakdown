## Quick Answer

**A progressive tax system charges higher tax rates on higher levels of income.** The United States uses this approach for federal income tax, with seven [tax brackets](/learn/what-are-tax-brackets/) ranging from 10% to 37%. The key feature is that only the income within each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate — not your entire income. This means your [effective tax rate](/learn/what-is-effective-tax-rate/) is always lower than your [marginal rate](/learn/what-is-marginal-tax-rate/).

## How Progressive Taxation Works

Imagine the tax system as filling up buckets. Your first dollars of income fill the lowest-rate bucket (10%). Once that bucket is full, additional income spills into the next bucket (12%), then the next (22%), and so on.

For a single filer in 2025:

| Bucket | Income Range | Rate |
|--------|-------------|------|
| First | $0 - $11,925 | 10% |
| Second | $11,926 - $48,475 | 12% |
| Third | $48,476 - $103,350 | 22% |
| Fourth | $103,351 - $197,300 | 24% |
| Fifth | $197,301 - $250,525 | 32% |
| Sixth | $250,526 - $626,350 | 35% |
| Seventh | $626,351+ | 37% |

This design ensures that people who earn less keep a larger percentage of their income, while those who earn more contribute a larger share.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Compare two workers, both single filers in [Texas](/texas/) (no state tax), both taking the $15,000 [standard deduction](/learn/what-is-standard-deduction/):

**Alex earns $45,000** (taxable income: $30,000)
- 10% on first $11,925 = $1,192.50
- 12% on remaining $18,075 = $2,169.00
- **Total tax: $3,361.50** — Effective rate: **11.2%**

**Jordan earns $120,000** (taxable income: $105,000)
- 10% on first $11,925 = $1,192.50
- 12% on $36,550 = $4,386.00
- 22% on $54,875 = $12,072.50
- 24% on $1,650 = $396.00
- **Total tax: $18,047.00** — Effective rate: **17.2%**

Jordan earns 2.67x more than Alex but pays 5.4x more in federal tax. That is progressive taxation in action. See your own effective rate using the [SalaryHog calculator](/).

## The Common Misconception

The biggest misunderstanding about progressive taxes is that "moving into a higher bracket" means all your income gets taxed at the new rate. This is completely false. If Alex gets a raise from $45,000 to $50,000, only the $5,000 in additional income enters the next bracket — the tax on the first $45,000 stays exactly the same. A raise always increases your [take-home pay](/learn/what-is-net-pay/). Learn more about [how raises are taxed](/learn/how-raises-are-taxed/).

## Progressive vs Other Systems

Not all taxes are progressive:

- **Progressive**: Federal income tax — higher rates on higher income
- **Flat**: Some [state income taxes](/learn/states-with-flat-income-tax/) charge a single rate regardless of income. See the [flat vs progressive comparison](/learn/flat-tax-vs-progressive-tax/)
- **Regressive**: Sales tax and [Social Security tax](/learn/what-is-social-security-tax/) take a larger percentage from lower earners

## Why It Matters for Planning

Understanding progressive taxation helps you make smart financial moves:

- **Retirement contributions**: Putting money into a [401(k)](/learn/how-401k-reduces-taxes/) shaves income from your highest bracket first
- **State selection**: Moving from [California](/california/) (progressive state tax up to 13.3%) to [Florida](/florida/) (no state tax) can dramatically change your rate. Compare with the [relocation tool](/relocate/)
- **Filing jointly**: [Married filing jointly](/learn/married-filing-jointly-explained/) widens the brackets, potentially lowering your effective rate. Try the [married calculator](/married/)

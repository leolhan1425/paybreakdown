We ran $75,000 through every state's tax code — federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security, and Medicare — to see exactly how much you take home depending on where you live. Single filer, standard deduction, 2025 brackets. The gap between the best and worst state is $6,254 a year, which is roughly the cost of a decent vacation you're either taking or not taking based entirely on which side of a state line your apartment sits on.

## The top and bottom aren't where you'd expect

The nine states with no state income tax — [Alaska](/alaska/), [Florida](/florida/), [Nevada](/nevada/), [New Hampshire](/new-hampshire/), [South Dakota](/south-dakota/), [Tennessee](/tennessee/), [Texas](/texas/), [Washington](/washington/), and [Wyoming](/wyoming/) — are all tied at exactly $61,149. That's not a rounding artifact. When there's no state tax, the only deductions are federal income tax ($7,660), Social Security ($4,650), and Medicare ($1,088), and those are identical regardless of geography. If you're comparing these nine states to each other, taxes aren't the variable — cost of living is. A dollar goes a lot further in Cheyenne than it does in Seattle.

The surprise is at the bottom. [Oregon](/oregon/) finishes dead last at $54,895, and it's not particularly close — $1,399 behind [Maine](/maine/) in 50th place. Oregon's progressive income tax reaches a top marginal rate of 9.9%, which kicks in at just $125,000 for single filers. That means at $75,000 you're already in the 8.75% bracket, which is higher than what most people pay in [California](/california/) at the same income. People tend to think of California as the highest-taxed state, but California's graduated brackets are actually more generous at middle incomes — a $75K earner in California takes home $57,631, nearly $2,700 more than in Oregon.

## The middle of the pack is shockingly tight

Here's the thing that jumped out when we looked at the full ranking: the difference between the 15th-ranked state and the 35th-ranked state is only about $800 a year. That's $67 a month. States like [Pennsylvania](/pennsylvania/) (#15, $58,846), [Connecticut](/connecticut/) (#30, $57,774), and [California](/california/) (#34, $57,631) are functionally identical in terms of take-home pay at this salary level. All the heated arguments about which of these states is "actually cheaper" are arguments about the equivalent of a single nice dinner out per month.

The reason the middle is so compressed is that most state income taxes at $75,000 cluster between 3% and 5% effective rates. The states that break away from the pack are either the ones charging nothing (the no-tax nine at the top) or the ones with unusually aggressive rates at middle incomes (Oregon at the bottom, with [Maine](/maine/), [District of Columbia](/district-of-columbia/), [Minnesota](/minnesota/), and [Hawaii](/hawaii/) not far behind).

## Flat tax vs. progressive tax — the color tells the story

In the chart below, color represents the type of state income tax: **green** for no state income tax, **blue** for a flat rate, and **orange** for progressive brackets. One thing that immediately stands out is that flat-tax states (blue) are scattered all over the ranking, not bunched at the top or bottom. [North Dakota](/north-dakota/) with its 1.95% flat rate lands at #10, basically indistinguishable from the no-tax states. But [Illinois](/illinois/) with its 4.95% flat rate ends up at #37. A flat tax doesn't inherently mean low taxes — it means everyone pays the same rate, and whether that rate is generous or punishing depends entirely on the number the state legislature picked.

Progressive-tax states (orange) have the widest spread. [Ohio](/ohio/) at #11 uses progressive brackets that barely touch a $75K salary, while [Oregon](/oregon/) at #51 uses progressive brackets that hit the same salary hard. The structure matters less than the rates within the structure.

## How we calculated this

Every figure uses 2025 tax year data: federal progressive brackets (10% to 37%) applied after the standard deduction of $15,000, Social Security at 6.2% on income up to $176,100, Medicare at 1.45% on all income, and each state's current income tax rates and brackets. Filing status is single throughout.

What this does **not** include: local or city income taxes (which would hurt [New York City](/new-york/) residents significantly), property taxes, sales taxes, or any cost-of-living adjustment. This is purely paycheck math — what hits your bank account before you spend a dime on rent, groceries, or gas.

## What about cost of living?

This is the question that comes up every time someone posts a state tax comparison, and it's a fair one. Take-home pay is only half the equation. [Tennessee](/tennessee/) and [Washington](/washington/) both show $61,149, but the median rent in Nashville is roughly half what it is in Seattle. A cost-of-living-adjusted version of this chart would look dramatically different, and we're working on exactly that — the [relocation calculator](/relocate/) already handles metro-to-metro comparisons if you want to run specific scenarios.

But raw take-home pay still matters. It's the starting point for every other financial decision you make, and most people have never actually looked up what their state takes before they see their first paycheck.

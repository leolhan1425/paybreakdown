## Quick Answer

**The states with the highest income tax rates in 2025 include California (up to 13.3%), Hawaii (up to 11%), New Jersey (up to 10.75%), Oregon (up to 9.9%), and Minnesota (up to 9.85%).** Living in a high-tax state can cost you $5,000 to $15,000 more per year compared to a [no-income-tax state](/learn/states-with-no-income-tax/) like [Texas](/texas/) or [Florida](/florida/), depending on your income level.

## Real Example With Actual Numbers

Compare a $120,000 salary across high-tax and low-tax states (single filer):

| State | State Income Tax | Take-Home Difference vs Texas |
|-------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| [California](/california/) | ~$6,300 | -$6,300 |
| [New York](/new-york/) (state only) | ~$6,100 | -$6,100 |
| New York City (state + city) | ~$9,800 | -$9,800 |
| Oregon | ~$8,400 | -$8,400 |
| Minnesota | ~$6,900 | -$6,900 |
| [Texas](/texas/) | $0 | Baseline |
| [Florida](/florida/) | $0 | Same as Texas |

Someone in New York City earning $120,000 pays nearly $10,000 more in state and local income taxes than someone in Texas. That is over $800/month. Compare your specific scenario with the [relocation tool](/relocate/).

## The Hidden Costs Beyond the Top Rate

The top rate only tells part of the story. What matters more for most workers is the **effective state tax rate** at their income level:

| State | Rate on $75K Income | Rate on $200K Income |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|
| California | ~4.3% | ~6.8% |
| New York | ~5.2% | ~6.2% |
| Oregon | ~8.5% | ~9.2% |
| Minnesota | ~6.0% | ~8.0% |

Oregon stands out because its high rate kicks in at a relatively low income ($125,000), meaning middle-class earners feel the full impact. California's high rates only apply above $1 million, so most workers pay a more moderate effective rate.

## Additional State-Level Taxes

Some high-tax states pile on extra costs:

- **California SDI**: 1.1% on all wages
- **New York City tax**: Up to 3.876% on top of state tax
- **New Jersey**: Has both high income tax and the highest property taxes in the nation
- **Oregon**: No sales tax, which partially offsets the income tax

## Should You Move?

Relocating from a [high-tax state](/learn/highest-tax-states/) to a [no-tax state](/learn/states-with-no-income-tax/) can yield significant savings, but consider:

- **Cost of living**: Housing in [Florida](/florida/) and [Texas](/texas/) varies widely by city
- **Career opportunities**: High-tax states often have higher salaries to compensate
- **Quality of services**: Higher taxes often fund better public schools, infrastructure, and services
- **Remote work**: If you can keep a high-tax-state salary while living elsewhere, the savings are maximized. See [remote work tax rules](/learn/working-remotely-across-state-lines/)
